Modifiable vs. Non-modifiable Risk Factors

  Module 3

Risk factors for cancer are classified as non-modifiable and modifiable:

Non-modifiable risk factors for cancer include things that cannot be changed:

  • Some exposures from work related exposures
  • Biological factors such as genetics, age, gender, race

Modifiable cancer risk factors are factors that can be changed:

  • Some exposures from work related exposures
  • Biological factors such as genetics, age, gender, race

A complex interaction between these non-modifiable and modifiable risk factors is what determines overall cancer risk.

As there are some risk factors you cannot change as a fire fighter, a greater emphasis must be placed on changing the modifiable risk factors in your control to help counteract the non-modifiable ones.

In a recent analysis, the modifiable risk factors of body weight, physical activity, diet, and alcohol intake accounted for at least 18.2% of cancer cases and 15.8% of cancer deaths. Therefore, targeting these behaviors has the potential to reduce overall cancer risk.

The American Cancer Society (ACS) provides specific recommendations for lifestyle health behaviors for cancer prevention.

Is sleep important for cancer prevention?

  Module 3

Disordered sleep has been associated with higher cancer risk, and sleep deprivation (7 hours per night) has been associated with a higher risk for obesity which increases cancer risk.

A healthy sleep pattern has been linked to improved weight maintenance after weight loss.

High levels of sedentary time have been associated with poor sleep quality and shortened sleep duration.

Inadequate sleep has been demonstrated to increase stress hormone level and inflammation which both increase cancer risk.

Should I buy organic or conventional produce?

  • Current scientific evidence supports the overall health benefits and cancer protective effects of eating vegetables and fruit, regardless of whether they are grown using organic or conventional practices.
  • Therefore, meeting vegetable and fruit intake recommendation intake is a higher priority over choosing organic produce.
  • Washing conventionally grown produce can remove some of the pesticide residue and minimize the risk of microbial contamination.

Does sugar increase cancer risk?

  Module 3

Sugar comes in many forms although they all have similar metabolic effects.

Sugar intake can increase cancer risk indirectly through promoting obesity.

Diet patterns high in sugar can affect insulin and hormone levels which may increase cancer risk.

Studies have shown that glucose metabolism occurs more rapidly in cancer cells when compared to normal cells.

Limiting high sugar intake and highly processed foods such as cake, candy, cookies, cereals, and sugar sweetened beverages (soda, juice, sports and energy drinks) is therefore recommended for maintenance of a healthy body weight and minimizing cancer risk.

Do sugar substitutes cause cancer?

  Module 3

There are various non-nutritive sweeteners or sugar substitutes that are made contain few or no calories yet sweeter than sugar.

The sugar substitute saccharin was shown to cause bladder cancer in lab animals, although human studies did not support this finding.

Sugar substitutes if at all should be used only in moderation.

Cooking and Food Storage Techniques for Cancer Prevention

  Module 3

Do certain cooking methods or materials increase cancer risk?

  • Grilling, smoking, or pan-frying meats at high temperatures can cause chemical reactions in the food forming carcinogenic compounds.
  • Use of Teflon-coated cookware may release carcinogens into foods.

Do food preservation techniques influence cancer risk?

  • Certain methods of preserving meats cause nitrates to form which can be carcinogenic.

What containers should I use for food storage?

  • Plastic containers can release carcinogenic chemicals such as phthalates or phenolic compounds such as bisphenol A (BPA) during the storage of food and cooking of food in a microwave which can increase long term cancer risk.
  • To minimize risk of exposure glass or metal storage containers and cookware can be used.

Is a juice cleanse/detox beneficial for cancer prevention?

  Module 3

Toxins enter the body through the foods and beverages we consume and are eliminated by the liver and kidneys.

Fruit and vegetable juices can be used as a convenient way of consuming the beneficial bioactive food components and micronutrients in vegetables and fruit and can in this way serve as part of healthy diet.

Yet juices contain more sugar and less fiber than fruits and vegetables in their natural state thus whole foods are more beneficial than juices.

Furthermore, evidence does not support that consuming only juices for a time period reduces cancer risk.

#1 Achieve and maintain a healthy body weight

In a review of the most recent research excess body fat and weight gain in adulthood increases the risk for the following cancers:

Breast, Colorectal, Endometrial, Gallbladder, Kidney, Liver, Ovary, Pancreas, Prostate, Thyroid, Stomach/gastric, Upper digestive

Dietary factors associated with excess body fat include:

  • Excess sugar (such as sugar sweetened beverages and processed foods)
  • “Fast foods” and a “Western” type diet (high in added sugars, meat and fat)

Mediterranean style diet rich in fiber is associated with healthy body weight and cancer risk reduction.

Vegetarian / Vegan Diet

  • Vegetarian diets tend to be health promoting through being high in fiber, vitamins, and bioactive nutrients while low in saturated fat and not including red and processed meats.
  • For these reasons, it is possible that vegetarian diets can reduce cancer risk.
  • It is unclear if vegetarian diets have exclusive cancer prevention benefits over diets consisting of mostly plant-based foods with small amounts of animal products.
  • The vegan diet is a strict vegetarian diet that excludes all animal products including eggs and milk and therefore requires supplementation of the key- nutrients vitamin B12, zinc, calcium and iron.

#2 Be physically active

Obtaining regular moderate to vigorous physical activity and reducing sedentary time has been shown to lower the risk for the following cancers:

Breast, Colorectal, Endometrial, Kidney, Liver, Lung, Ovary, Pancreas, Stomach/gastric, Upper digestive

Adults should engage in moderate intensity physical activity for 150 to 300 minutes per week or 75 to 150 minutes of vigorous physical activity or a combination to reach equivalent.

Since a linear relationship is seen between physical activity time contribution and cancer prevention, obtaining 300 or more minutes of physical activity is optimal.

Limit sedentary activity such as sitting, laying down, and screen-based entertainment.

#3 Follow a healthy eating pattern

Following a healthy diet has been demonstrated to reduce the risk of the following cancers:
Breast, Colorectal, Endometrial, Liver, Lung, Ovary, Pancreas, Prostate, Stomach/gastric, Upper digestive.

A HEALTHY EATING PATTERN INCLUDES:

Foods that are nutrient dense and help achieve a healthy body weight

  • A variety of vegetables - dark green, red, and orange, fiber rich legumes (beans and peas), and others.
  • Fruits, especially whole fruits with a variety of colors
  • Whole grains

A healthy eating pattern limits or does not include:

  • Red and processed meats.
  • Sugar sweetened beverages
  • Highly processed foods and refined grains

In addition to eating patterns, specific nutrients can affect cancer risk

Calcium and dairy products

  • Excess calcium intake (>2g) increases risk for prostate cancer
  • Calcium intake may lower colorectal and breast cancer risk
  • Whole grains

Vitamin D

  • Most adults are insufficient in vitamin D (<50 nmol / L)
  • A clinical trial of supplementation of 2000iu/ day vitamin D lowered the risk of overall cancer mortality

Mediterranean Diet

  • A Mediterranean-style diet generally includes plentiful amounts of minimally processed plant-based foods including fruits, vegetables, whole grains potatoes, beans, nuts and seeds with olive oil as a primary fat source and low to moderate amounts of dairy products, eggs, poultry and fish.
  • Several studies have demonstrated the protective nature of a Mediterranean diet for cancer risk.
  • The possible mechanisms for this association include the diet’s high content of antioxidants (from colorful fruits and vegetables) and Omega 3 fatty acids and monounsaturated fats (from nuts/seeds, avocados and olive oil) with limited pro-inflammatory red meat and processed meat intake.
  • Additional lifestyle patterns included in the diet are moderate alcohol intake, and physical activity which have been shown to have independent cancer preventative effects.

#4 It is best to not drink alcohol

Alcohol consumption increases the risk for the following cancers: Breast, Colorectal, Kidney, Liver, Stomach/gastric, Upper digestive

A standard drink of alcohol is defined as:

  • 12 ounces of beer
  • Several studies have demonstrated the protective nature of a Mediterranean diet for cancer risk.
  • 5 ounces of wine
  • 1.5 ounces of 80-proof distilled spirits

People who chose to drink alcohol should limit consumption to no more than 1 drink per day for women and 2 drinks per day for men.

Binge drinking is defined as a drinking pattern that brings a person’s blood alcohol concentration (BAC) to 0.08 g/dl or above which is about 5 or more drinks for men and 4 or more drinks for women over the course of about 2 hours. Among other adverse health effects associated with binge drinking, an increased risk for cancer of the breast, mouth, throat, esophagus, liver, and colon.

Ketogenic Diet

  • The Ketogenic Diet (KD) is a high-fat , low-carbohydrate, moderate protein diet which was originally developed in the 1920s to treat epilepsy with a traditional macronutrient composition 90% of its calories from fat, 8% from protein, and 2% from carbohydrates.
  • However, various macronutrient composition with fat content of 50%-90% have been used as forms of a modified ketogenic diet.
  • The reduced intake of carbohydrates shifts energy production from glucose based to ketone based within about 1 week.
  • In cancer cells, most energy comes from glucose even in the presence of oxygen.
  • Therefore, the (KD) presents a possible opportunity to target the glucose dependent metabolic alterations in tumor cells thereby limiting tumor growth while protecting healthy cells.
  • While the (KD) is generally inexpensive, easy to implement, and fairly well tolerated, the research regarding this diet is focused on use of the diet as adjunctive to cancer treatment not for cancer prevention, therefore, further research is needed in this area.
