Case Study: What can be done differently?

  Module 3

At the station, a firefighter arrives to his shift and soon after walks to get some ice for a glass of water from the ice machine in the bay area. As the firefighter returns to the living quarters, the doors to the bay area are left open. While the fire fighter is discussing recent procedural changes with the team, a call is received. The team then prepares to answer the call. While the team finishes up, the engine is turned on before bay doors are open to help save some time. Once the team is ready and on the truck, they depart the station in route to the scene.

At the scene, the team dons gear properly and begins fire suppression activities. During fire suppression, team members are pulled from the fire to rehab. Once fire suppression activities are completed, the team then performs overhaul. Because the air is visibly clear, SCBA is removed during overhaul procedures. After completing overhaul, the team doffs gear and places the gear in the engine cab for the ride back to the station.

When the team arrives at the station, gear is placed in lockers in the bay area and the team then heads to the living quarters to rest as they wait for the next call.

Case Study: What can be done differently?

  Module 3

Doors between bay area and living quarters should always remain closed to avoid contaminating living quarters with diesel exhaust or other toxins
Bay doors should be open before starting engine to allow for proper ventilation.
Firefighters should be careful to complete some kind of decontamination prior to hydrating to avoid accidentally ingesting toxins.
SCBA should be worn during overhaul. Even though the air may appear clear, it may still contain toxins.
Post fire on-scene gross decontamination should be completed.
